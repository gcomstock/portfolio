import imgBroadNav from './images/broad-nav.gif';

<ImagePanel
  image={imgBroadNav}
  alt="Early wireframe defining page layouts and UX patterns for Spotlight"
  title="Defining Page Layouts and UX Patterns"
>
  <p>Because the project was new, there were no existing patterns to inherit. I started with low-fidelity Figma mocks to reach alignment, then designed a set of higher-fidelity exemplar plugins that became the reference standard for future plugin providers. Those patterns eventually lived in both a Figma component library and the codebase itself, with the goal that providers could make sound design decisions without needing me in the room.</p>
  <p>One point of contention was the detail panel: a right-side drawer that opened when a user clicked on an entity. Critics felt it obscured the main content area. The counter-argument was that once a user opened a detail panel, it became their primary area of focus. Keyboard shortcuts let them move between entities without ever returning to the list, making the main content area a secondary concern in that moment. And for larger viewports, a max-width constraint on the main content column meant the panel opened beside the content rather than over it.</p>
</ImagePanel>

<ImagePanel
  image={imgBroadIA}
  alt="Early wireframe establishing the information architecture for Spotlight"
  title="Establishing the Information Architecture"
  reverse
>
  <p>Existing developer tools at Netflix were inconsistent. Some let you view specific applications or teams, some didn't. Some let you save entities, some didn't. The IA work aimed to bring enough consistency that any plugin provider could develop against it and have their experience feel seamless alongside every other plugin.</p>
  <p>We landed on a sidebar for top-level plugin navigation, keeping traversal simple regardless of how many plugins existed. Within a plugin, breadcrumbs handled deeper navigation, but switching plugins reset the breadcrumb trail. A global search tied it all together, letting users jump directly to an application, team, or collection and have the sidebar reflect their context automatically. The implicit promise to plugin providers: build inside Spotlight, and all of this comes for free.</p>
</ImagePanel>

  <p>User experiences around software discovery and the software catalog were already well on the way from before our priorities were narrowed. Because there were no existing teams within our org that handled campaigns, that was on us now. I conducted a series of customer interviews and put together low-fidelity mocks to cater to two different personas. The campaign manager and the campaign recipient.</p>
  <p>Campaigns would be the first major feature available in Spotlight. Campaign owners could create migration plans or library upgrades and target them against the entire software catalog. Microservice owners could then pick up those campaigns directly in Spotlight. Campaign progress was tracked through a structured set of statuses: NOT_STARTED, BLOCKED, IN_PROGRESS, WILL_NOT_DO, and COMPLETE. This gave campaign managers the data they needed to build timelines, make predictions, and have honest conversations about what was actually achievable.</p>
  <p>The MVP focused on visibility into microservice hygiene, but the vision didn't stop there. Over time it evolved into guided wizards and even automated campaigns, where microservice owners simply had to acknowledge the change.</p>

<section id="solution">

## Solution

<ProductPanel images={[imgSolOverview1, imgSolOverview2]} alt="Spotlight Software Overview" title="Software Overview">
  <p>Spotlight became the source of truth for software ownership at Netflix. Anyone could look up who owned a given service, and whether it was actively maintained or inactive, solving a long-standing problem where abandoned services had no clear owner and dependencies felt like a minefield. Teams also had a dedicated view of everything they owned, along with campaign completion rates to track progress against active migrations and upgrades. Broader SDLC integrations were deliberately deferred, surfaced for now as simple links out to the existing products teams were already using, keeping the scope focused while the foundation matured.</p>
</ProductPanel>

<ProductPanel images={[imgSolPaved1, imgSolPaved2]} alt="Spotlight Paved Road" title="The Paved Road">
  <p>A curated directory of platform-approved solutions for common development patterns at Netflix. Instead of asking around or reinventing approaches already solved elsewhere, engineers could browse recommended starters, templates, and libraries organized by use case. For new hires especially, it replaced a steep, unguided ramp with a clear and opinionated path forward.</p>
</ProductPanel>

<ProductPanel images={[imgSolCampaign1, imgSolCampaign2]} alt="Spotlight Campaigns" title="Campaigns">
  <p>A coordination tool for driving large-scale migrations and upgrades across Netflix's microservice catalog. Campaign owners define the work, target affected services, and track completion through a structured set of statuses. Microservice owners receive clear, actionable requests directly in Spotlight, giving both sides real data to build timelines, set expectations, and have honest conversations about what was achievable.</p>
</ProductPanel>
